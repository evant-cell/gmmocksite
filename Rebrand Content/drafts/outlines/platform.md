# Outline, /platform (Platform hub)

**Page job.** Establish that Genius Monkey runs a real programmatic advertising platform, its own DSP with experts behind it, and route visitors to the seven Platform sub-pages. One action: request a demo.

**Page type.** Service page structure. Word target: 1,200 to 1,600.

**Keywords** (from page-plan.md). Primary: programmatic advertising platform (600/KD9). Aspirational head: programmatic advertising (7900/KD30). Secondary: managed programmatic advertising, programmatic advertising company, demand side platform. Primary term in the H1 and first 100 words, used naturally.

**Meta title (draft).** Programmatic Advertising Platform | Genius Monkey
**Meta description (draft, under 155 chars).** Genius Monkey runs its own DSP with a real expert behind every account. Transparent reporting, cross-device reach, results you can actually explain.

**Message map.** The visitor must believe, in order: (1) GM runs its own platform, not a reseller layer. (2) It is managed, real experts, not a dashboard dumped on you. (3) It measures honestly, every result can be seen and explained. (4) It performs, with proof. (5) A demo is the obvious next step.

## Sections

### 1. Hero
- H1 (draft copy): **The programmatic advertising platform with real experts behind it**
- Lead (draft copy): "Genius Monkey runs its own demand-side platform and puts a real person behind every account. You see every dollar, every placement, and what actually moved the number. No guesswork, no black boxes."
- Primary CTA: Request a demo.

### 2. Definition block (AEO)
- Answers "What is a programmatic advertising platform?" in one extractable 40 to 60 word block, placed high on the page.
- Draft copy (47 words): "A programmatic advertising platform buys digital ad space automatically, using real-time data to decide which impressions are worth your money. Genius Monkey runs its own demand-side platform and pairs it with real experts, so campaigns are targeted precisely and measured transparently, then adjusted the moment performance shifts."
- Link the term DSP to the glossary entry; the glossary links back.

### 3. The problem, in the visitor's words
- Intent: name the pain before pitching. Buyer is spending real money on programmatic somewhere and cannot explain the results.
- Lead (draft copy): "You are spending real money and getting reports you can't explain. Somewhere between your budget and the screen, the story goes dark."
- Contents: two or three short paragraphs. The black-box problem (self-serve DSPs and reseller stacks), the reporting problem, the nobody-owns-it problem.

### 4. What the platform does (routes to the seven sub-pages)
- Intent: scannable capability section. Each capability is a short teaser plus a link to its sub-page. Order follows the buyer's questions, how it works, then targeting, then proof of performance.
- Section header (draft): **What you get, and where to dig in**
- The seven teasers (each 2 to 3 sentences at draft stage, one-line intents here):
  - How it works. The buying process end to end, plain words. Links /platform/how-it-works.
  - Real-time optimization. Campaigns adjust while they run, not at the monthly review. Links /platform/real-time-optimization.
  - Device-agnostic targeting. Reach the same person across every screen they use. Links /platform/device-agnostic-targeting.
  - Behavioral targeting. Audiences built on what people do, not who a data broker says they are. Links /platform/behavioral-targeting.
  - Measurement and validation. Attribution that tells the true story of what worked. Links /platform/measurement-validation.
  - Human oversight. A real expert on your account who treats your budget like their own. Links /platform/human-oversight.
  - The dashboard. Every placement, every dollar, visible whenever you want it. Links /platform/the-dashboard.

### 5. Managed platform vs self-serve DSP (AEO comparison)
- Answers the plan's second AEO question, "How is a managed platform different from a self-serve DSP?" Structure as a short comparison table (rows: who runs campaigns, who optimizes, reporting, what you pay for) plus one direct-answer paragraph a machine can lift whole.
- Lead (draft copy): "A self-serve DSP hands you the keys and wishes you luck. A managed platform comes with the people who drive it. Genius Monkey is the second kind, on technology we own."

### 6. Proof
- Intent: hard numbers from the cleared ledger only, each with its case study linked.
- Use (from brand/facts.md): onsemi, cost per conversion from $2,000 to under $1. Country Thunder, 2300% return on ad spend, 43% of ticket buyers brand new. Link both case studies and the Case Studies hub.
- Lead (draft copy): "onsemi came to us with a $2,000 cost per conversion. It's now under a dollar. Country Thunder turned every ad dollar into $230 of ticket sales. The receipts are in the case studies."

### 7. FAQ
- Real buyer objections, phrased as people ask them. Answers drafted at page-draft stage, each 40 to 60 words, first sentence standalone.
  - What is a programmatic advertising platform?
  - How is a managed platform different from a self-serve DSP?
  - Does Genius Monkey run its own DSP?
  - How much does programmatic advertising cost? (short answer, link /pricing)
  - How do I know it's working? (link /platform/measurement-validation)

### 8. Closing CTA
- Draft copy: "See the platform with your goals in it. Request a demo."
